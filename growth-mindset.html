Long-form Essay

The Updater

On growth mindset, neuroplasticity, and why the people who keep getting better are doing something the rest of us aren't.

Arvind Kumar Venugopal  ·  CS Lead, Accenture

In 1994, a psychologist named Claudia Mueller walked into a fifth-grade classroom in New York with a sheet of puzzles. The children solved them easily. Then Mueller did something that would divide the room in two. She praised half the children for how smart they were. She praised the other half for how hard they had worked. Then she gave everyone a second set of puzzles, much harder. Both groups struggled. Both groups got things wrong.

What happened next is one of the most quietly devastating findings in modern psychology.

The children praised for their intelligence, when faced with difficulty, began to avoid harder problems. They lied about their scores. Their performance, over subsequent weeks, deteriorated. The children praised for their effort did the opposite. They leaned in. They tried harder problems. Their scores improved. The gap between the two groups, over time, became almost impossible to close.

Same classroom. Same puzzles. Two sentences of feedback. Two completely different lives set in motion.

Carol Dweck, who supervised that research at Stanford, spent the next three decades trying to understand why. What she found was not a theory about praise or parenting. It was something much larger: a fundamental difference in how human beings relate to the idea of their own potential. Some people, Dweck found, believe their abilities are fixed. Given at birth, confirmed by performance, essentially unchangeable. Others believe their abilities can be developed. That effort, learning, and persistence can genuinely make them more capable than they were before.

She called these orientations fixed mindset and growth mindset.

The corporate world discovered this research around 2007 and did what the corporate world does: it flattened it into a workshop, laminated it into a poster, and asked managers to mention it in performance reviews. Somewhere between Dweck's lab and your company's HR deck, one of the most important ideas in modern psychology became the kind of thing HR talks about in April.

This essay is not about that version.

Consider two people starting the same job on the same day.

Both are talented. Both are ambitious. Within six months, one of them is visibly improving, taking on harder problems, asking more questions, looking slightly different from the person who walked in. The other is performing adequately, maybe well, but is recognisably the same person. Not worse. Just... the same.

Most organisations explain this gap with talent, or luck, or the quality of their manager. Dweck's research suggests the explanation runs deeper. The person who is changing has a fundamentally different relationship with difficulty than the person who isn't. Difficulty, for one of them, is information. For the other, it is judgment.

When difficulty is judgment, every hard moment is a verdict on who you are. You avoid the harder problems because getting them wrong would confirm the thing you fear most: that you're not as capable as people think. You perform. You protect. You stay in the range where you know you can succeed.

When difficulty is information, every hard moment is data. You got it wrong? Interesting. What does that tell you? You feel stupid? Good. That's what it feels like to be at the edge of what you currently know. The edge is the only place growth actually happens.

This is, at its core, what growth mindset means: getting pleasure from changing for the better rather than from being seen as already good. Not hustle. Not resilience, necessarily. Just the quiet, stubborn preference for internal feedback over external validation.

"The difficulty lies not in the new ideas, but in escaping the old ones, which ramify, for those brought up as most of us have been, into every corner of our minds."

John Maynard Keynes

The brain, it turns out, cooperates with this orientation in ways that were not understood until recently. Neuroscientists studying neuroplasticity, the brain's ability to physically rewire itself in response to experience, have established something remarkable: the adult brain is not fixed. It retains, across a lifetime, the capacity to form new neural pathways. Challenge, difficulty, repeated practice: these do not just improve performance. They change the physical structure of the organ doing the thinking. "You can change" is not a motivational slogan. It is a biological description of what happens every time you push past what you currently know.

But here is where Dweck herself issued a warning that most people have missed.

The trap Dweck named

In her later work, Dweck described what she called "false growth mindset": the performance of positivity without any of the substance. Telling yourself you can improve while changing nothing about the quality of challenge you pursue, the honesty of the feedback you seek, or the safety of the environment you operate in. Growth mindset without real difficulty, honest feedback, and psychological safety is not growth mindset. It is, as one researcher put it, optimism cosplay. The distinction matters enormously for everything that follows.

There is also a version of this idea that popular culture has almost entirely missed. Growth mindset is not a global personality trait you either have or don't have. It is a spectrum, and it is domain-specific. You can be deeply growth-oriented in your professional skills and carry a thoroughly fixed mindset about your relationships, your body, your creative ability. Sometimes in the same afternoon. The person who reads every book on leadership but cannot hear feedback from their partner without becoming defensive. The professional who welcomes stretch assignments at work and refuses to try anything new in their personal life.

The goal is not to "achieve" growth mindset. The goal is to notice, domain by domain, where the fixed version is quietly running the show.

Amy Edmondson arrived at Harvard Business School in the 1990s intending to study something different. She was researching medical errors in hospital teams, and she expected to find that the best teams made the fewest mistakes. What she found instead stopped her cold.

The best teams, by every measure of quality and patient outcomes, reported more errors, not fewer.

Edmondson spent years trying to understand this paradox. Eventually she realised she had been asking the wrong question. The best teams were not making more mistakes. They were reporting more mistakes, because they worked in an environment where it was safe to do so. They surfaced problems early. They spoke up about concerns. They learned from every near-miss rather than hiding it. The weaker teams were suppressing their errors, and the errors were consequently compounding in silence.

She called the condition she was studying psychological safety: the shared belief within a group that it is safe to take interpersonal risks, to speak up, to try things, to fail without punishment or humiliation.

This finding has enormous implications for how we think about growth mindset, and almost nobody talks about it. You cannot build a growth orientation inside an environment that punishes the behaviour growth requires. If you are trying to grow in a workplace, a relationship, or a community that rewards performance and penalises mistakes, the problem is not your mindset. It is the container. Changing yourself without examining the environment you are in is the most common way growth efforts quietly fail.

Before the habits, before the pillars, before any of the practices that follow: look at your container.

Part Two

Seven Ways to Actually Live It

Understanding growth mindset is not the same as having it. The gap between knowing and living is where most people get stuck forever. What follows are seven practices, not seven facts. Each one changes something. Each one has a first step small enough to take today.

One

Run the Show Reel

Naval Ravikant has a practice he calls unconditioning. The idea is simple and almost no one does it. Take your habits apart, one by one, and ask a question that sounds obvious but rarely gets asked: does this still serve me?

Not did it ever serve me. Not does someone else think it serves me. Does it serve me, now, as the person I am actually trying to become?

Most people, Ravikant argues, are running operating systems they never chose. The habits formed in childhood to get a parent's attention. The reactions learned in past relationships. The professional persona assembled in the first job, reinforced over years, eventually mistaken for character. We call it "who we are." Most of it is just old software that no one bothered to update.

Indranil Chakraborty offers a concrete tool for this audit. He calls it the show reel: run the significant moments of your life through your mind's eye. Where did you see your greatest success or failure? When did you feel genuinely, fully proud? What are the things you love, and why do you love them? What were the turning points, the moments where something shifted?

The answers to these questions tell you more about your actual values, your actual operating system, than any personality assessment ever will.

Spend ten minutes today writing answers to three questions: Where have I seen my greatest success or failure? When did I last feel genuinely proud? What do I love doing, and why? Write for yourself, not for anyone else. See what comes up.
Two

You Get What You Repeat

James Clear says something in Atomic Habits that takes about ten seconds to read and weeks to fully absorb: your outcomes are lagging measures of your habits.

Your knowledge is a lagging measure of your learning habits. Your net worth is a lagging measure of your financial habits. Your health is a lagging measure of your physical habits. Not your intentions. Not your self-image. Your habits. What you actually do, repeatedly, every day, regardless of whether you feel like it.

But Clear's deeper insight, the one that gets lost in the productivity conversation, is that lasting habit change is really identity change. You do not just build a new behaviour. At some point, you start to see yourself differently. As a learner. As an experimenter. As someone for whom difficulty is data rather than verdict. That shift in self-concept is what makes behaviours stick when motivation fades. The habit is the evidence. The identity is the infrastructure.

Anne-Laure Le Cunff adds a philosophy that completes this: the moment you stop measuring progress by outcomes and start measuring it by process, something changes. Success stops being a fixed target and becomes an unfolding path. You stop fixating on the destination and start following what genuinely captures your attention. The direction emerges organically. This sounds passive. It is not. It is, in fact, the only approach that compounds.

Pick one habit you want to build. Reduce it to its smallest possible daily form. Do it once, right now, to start the streak. The size does not matter. The repetition does.
Three

Start Loving Problems

Brad Jacobs built and sold multiple billion-dollar companies. When asked what the common thread was, he said something that sounds paradoxical until you sit with it: the secret is learning to love problems.

Not tolerate them. Not manage them. Love them. Because each problem is evidence that something is improvable. Each problem is an obstacle that, once removed, gets you measurably closer to where you want to be. The people who build the most, in business and in life, are the ones who have trained themselves to feel a flicker of something like curiosity or even excitement when something goes wrong, instead of dread.

Ambi Parameswaran gives this idea two words worth keeping. He calls difficult situations "probortunities" and rejections "rejbortunities." The language is playful, but the mechanism is serious. Every rejection opens a window, if you are looking for the window. Every problem contains the seed of a solution, if your orientation is toward finding it rather than surviving it.

This orientation is trainable. Noah Kagan describes it as staying perpetually alert: always watching for problems as opportunities, always beginning experiments, always asking the question that unlocks the next move. Not naive positivity. A discipline.

Take your current biggest obstacle and write it as a question beginning with "How might I..." The reframe alone will change what you see when you look at it.
Four

The Skill of Learning Itself

Cal Newport makes an observation in Deep Work that is easy to nod at and difficult to actually reckon with: the technologies and skills that matter in the modern economy change rapidly. Not once. Not occasionally. Continuously. The ability to master hard things quickly, and then to do it again and again as the terrain shifts, is not a nice-to-have skill. It is the skill. Everything else is built on top of it.

Darius Foroux takes this further with a question that reorients the entire way most people think about their careers: stop measuring your professional life by income or status. Measure it by this instead: have you reached your full learning potential? It is a question almost no one asks. It is also the only question that matters for the long game.

Tiago Forte adds the ingredient that makes learning sustainable: fascination. Surround yourself with ideas, people, and problems that provoke genuine curiosity, and you will not need to motivate yourself to learn. The emotion pulls you forward. Discipline is what you use when fascination runs out. The goal is to keep fascination running.

Learn one thing today. A chapter, a video, a conversation with someone who knows something you don't. The topic matters less than the habit. Do it before you sleep tonight.
Five

Make Good Mistakes

The philosopher Daniel Dennett draws a distinction that sounds like wordplay until you realise it is actually engineering. He distinguishes between bad mistakes, which are careless and teach you nothing, and good mistakes, ones made in genuine pursuit of something difficult, that prompt you to reflect, refine your approach, and increase your momentum. The goal, he argues, is not to avoid mistakes. The goal is to make good ones.

Sönke Ahrens, writing about the science of learning, goes further: actively seeking and welcoming feedback, positive or negative, is one of the most important predictors of long-term success and happiness that research has found. Not tolerating feedback. Not accepting it graciously when it arrives. Seeking it. Going looking for the information that tells you where you are wrong.

This requires a framework, because not all criticism is useful and not all feedback deserves equal weight. Dr. Julie Smith offers one: tolerate useful criticism and let it genuinely help you. Learn from negative feedback that moves you forward. Discard criticisms that reflect someone else's values rather than your own. And get clear on whose opinions actually matter to you, so you know when to reflect and when to let go and move on.

Do ten repetitions of something you are afraid to fail at. Call them practice, not attempts. Count the reps. Notice what changes by the tenth one.
Six

Hypothesise. Test. Adjust.

Anne-Laure Le Cunff has a line that is worth reading slowly: approach your life with the humility of a scientist, not the rigidity of an officer following orders.

An officer executes a plan. Regardless of what the ground is telling them, regardless of what the results are showing, they keep executing. We call this discipline. Mostly it is stubbornness dressed in respectable clothing. A scientist, by contrast, has a hypothesis. They run an experiment. They read what the results actually say. They adjust. The scientist's approach looks softer. It produces more.

Michael Watkins formalises the cycle: collect information, distil it, develop a hypothesis, test it, deepen your understanding. Repeat indefinitely. Julie Zhuo puts the same idea in three lines: figure out what worked and what didn't. File away the lessons. Get better. Rinse and repeat.

This is not a framework for work. It is a framework for a life.

Pick one area where you have been rigid, where you have been executing a plan despite evidence it is not working. Define it as an experiment instead. What would you test first?
Seven

Replace the Second Arrow

There is a Buddhist teaching that Le Cunff returns to several times, and it is one of the most useful ideas in this entire subject. The first arrow is the difficult thing that happens to you: the failure, the mistake, the rejection, the gap between who you are and who you want to be. The first arrow often cannot be avoided. It hurts. That is the nature of growing.

The second arrow is the one you fire at yourself immediately after. The shame spiral. The "why am I like this" loop. The self-attack that turns a single bad moment into a sustained internal war. The second arrow, almost always, can be avoided. It is optional. It is chosen.

Research on self-compassion, led by Kristin Neff at the University of Texas, has established something important: treating yourself with the same kindness you would offer a good friend in difficulty does not make you soft or complacent. It makes you more resilient, more likely to try again, and more capable of learning from what went wrong. Harshness toward yourself does not improve performance. It impairs it.

The practical move is Le Cunff's: when you catch yourself firing the second arrow, replace it with curiosity. Not "why am I like this" but "that's interesting, what actually happened there?" The war with yourself ends. The experiment begins.

Next time you catch yourself being harsh about a mistake, ask one question: what would I say to a close friend in exactly this situation? Then say that to yourself, and mean it.
Part Three

What Two Years of This Actually Looks Like

Morgan Housel writes about money when he describes compounding, but the logic applies to every domain in which a small gain can serve as fuel for a larger one. The reason most people underestimate what is possible from a small start, he argues, is that they measure too early. They look at month one and extrapolate nothing. They miss year five entirely.

A person who reads consistently for two years does not just know more than they did. They think differently. The neural pathways built through sustained engagement with difficult ideas change how information is processed, how problems are framed, how quickly connections are made. A person who has sought feedback honestly for two years does not just improve faster. They are no longer afraid of judgment in the way they once were, and that change in their relationship with evaluation changes everything about how they move through the world.

This is the compound growth that growth mindset actually produces, and it is not visible in month one.

"A good half of the art of living is resilience."

Alain de Botton

Paul Millerd describes a transition he calls the second chapter of success. It is the shift, usually hard-won, from measuring your life by what you lack to recognising what you have to offer. From ambition (the hunger to achieve, to prove, to arrive) to aspiration (the desire to contribute something that matters, to build something beyond yourself). From hoping joy will appear when you hit a specific outcome, to discovering it as a byproduct of the journey itself.

Most people are still in chapter one, waiting for it to pay off. The second chapter opens the moment you decide that the process is the point.

Martin Seligman spent decades studying what he called learned optimism, and his findings do not read like self-help. They read like a medical report. Optimistic people, defined not as people who feel good but as people who have trained themselves toward a growth orientation, are physically healthier, less likely to suffer from clinical depression, more productive at work, and better performers in competition. The training, Seligman found, is the variable. It is not a personality type you are born with. It is a skill set. And like every skill set, it responds to practice.

Cal Newport names the final reward. When you treat your work as craft, when you hone your ability and apply it with genuine care, you generate meaning in the daily efforts of your professional life. Not in flashes. Not when the recognition arrives. Daily, accumulated, deposited. The skilled wheelwright, Newport writes, finds it not in the product but in the practice.

The Chain

Gandhi, as quoted by Haemin Sunim in The Things You Can See Only When You Slow Down, draws the line from today to everywhere in one sentence: keep your habits positive, because your habits become your values. Keep your values positive, because your values become your destiny.

This is not mysticism. It is mechanics. The habit you build today becomes the value you hold in five years. The value you hold in five years shapes every significant decision you make in ten. Growth mindset living is not about optimising any single area of your life. It is about tending the chain, daily, unglamorously, with curiosity instead of judgment, until the destination takes care of itself.

You do not need to believe you can become exceptional. You need only to be genuinely curious about what happens if you keep going.

The two groups of fifth-graders from Mueller's 1994 classroom are adults now. Their trajectories separated not because of talent or circumstance but because of two sentences spoken to them at age ten. Two different ways of understanding what difficulty means.

You get to choose which sentence you speak to yourself today.
